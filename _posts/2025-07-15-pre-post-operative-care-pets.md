Knowing what to expect before and after surgery significantly reduces anxiety — for you and your pet. At **Shivraj Pet Clinic & Lab**, we believe informed pet parents lead to better recovery outcomes. Here is a complete guide to pre and post-operative care.

## Before Surgery — Pre-Operative Care

### Pre-Anaesthetic Bloodwork

Before any surgery involving anaesthesia, we run a pre-anaesthetic blood panel (CBC + biochemistry). This checks liver and kidney function, clotting ability, and overall health status. Anaesthesia is metabolised by the liver and excreted by the kidneys — abnormal values change our anaesthetic protocol to keep your pet safe. This is not optional — it is how we make anaesthesia safe, particularly for senior pets and exotic animals.

### Fasting Requirements

**Dogs and cats:** Fast for 8–12 hours before surgery. Water is generally permitted until 2 hours before. A full stomach during anaesthesia risks regurgitation and aspiration pneumonia.

**Rabbits and exotic animals:** Do NOT fast rabbits, guinea pigs, or birds before surgery. Unlike dogs and cats, they cannot vomit, and fasting causes dangerous GI stasis and hypoglycaemia. Bring them in well-fed.

### Day of Surgery

Arrive at the appointed time. Bring your pet's previous medical records and any medications they are currently taking. Our team will recheck vitals before proceeding.

## During Surgery — What We Do

All surgeries at Shivraj Pet Clinic are performed in our dedicated surgical suite with:
- Gas anaesthesia (isoflurane) — safer and more controllable than injectable-only
- Continuous heart rate and oxygen saturation monitoring
- Sterile surgical instruments and draping
- Pain management administered during surgery so recovery is more comfortable

## After Surgery — Post-Operative Care at Home

### First 24 Hours

Your pet will be groggy from anaesthesia. This is normal. Keep them in a quiet, warm, confined space away from other pets and children. Do not leave them on elevated surfaces — they may fall. Offer a small amount of water first. If they keep it down, offer a small meal 4–6 hours after returning home.

### Wound Care

Check the surgical site twice daily for:
- Excessive swelling or redness beyond day 2
- Discharge that is yellow, green, or foul-smelling
- Wound opening or sutures coming loose
- Your pet excessively licking the wound

**Use the E-collar (cone) consistently** — it is the single most important tool for preventing wound complications. Pets lick wounds instinctively, and even one hour of licking can undo days of healing.

### Pain Management

We send pain medication home with your pet. Give it on schedule even if your pet appears comfortable — pain worsens when medication is allowed to wear off completely.

### Activity Restriction

Restrict activity for the period your vet specifies — typically 7–14 days for soft tissue surgeries and 4–8 weeks for orthopaedic procedures. No running, jumping, or climbing stairs. Leash walks only.

### Follow-Up Appointment

A post-operative check is typically scheduled at 7 days for suture inspection and 14 days for removal. Do not miss this — complications caught early are always easier to manage.
