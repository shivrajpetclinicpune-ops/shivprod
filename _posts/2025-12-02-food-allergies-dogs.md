Food allergy is one of the most misunderstood and underdiagnosed conditions in dogs. At **Shivraj Pet Clinic & Lab**, we regularly see dogs that have been itching and on repeated courses of antibiotics and steroids for months — when the underlying cause was a dietary protein their immune system had been reacting to for years.

## What is Food Allergy in Dogs?

Food allergy is an abnormal immune-mediated response to a protein in the diet. It is distinct from food intolerance (which is not immune-mediated and typically causes only gastrointestinal signs). True food allergy causes itching, skin disease, and sometimes gastrointestinal signs simultaneously.

Food allergy accounts for 10–15% of all allergic skin disease in dogs and can occur at any age — including in dogs that have been eating the same diet for years without previous reactions. The immune system becomes sensitised over months to years of repeated exposure.

## Most Common Food Allergens in Dogs

Despite popular belief, grains are rarely the problem. The most common food allergens in Indian dogs are:

1. **Chicken** — the most common food allergen in dogs, because it is the most commonly fed protein
2. **Beef** — second most common
3. **Dairy products** — particularly cheese and milk
4. **Wheat** — though less common than proteins
5. **Soy** — found in many commercial dog foods as a filler
6. **Lamb** — becoming more common as it is used as a "hypoallergenic" alternative
7. **Egg** — less common but documented

## Signs of Food Allergy in Dogs

- **Chronic itching** that does not respond to seasonal patterns (unlike pollen allergy, food allergy is year-round)
- **Recurring ear infections** — often the first sign
- **Recurring skin infections** (bacterial and yeast)
- **Foot licking** — similar to environmental atopy
- **Facial rubbing**
- **Gastrointestinal signs** — loose stools, increased frequency of bowel movements, flatulence, occasional vomiting
- **Poor response to steroid treatment** — steroids typically reduce environmental allergy itch by 80–90%; food allergy itch often only partially responds

## Diagnosis — The Elimination Diet Trial

There is no blood test or skin test that reliably diagnoses food allergy in dogs. The only reliable diagnostic tool is an **8–12 week dietary elimination trial**.

This requires:
- Feeding a protein and carbohydrate source your dog has **never eaten before** (novel protein diet — e.g., kangaroo, venison, rabbit, insect protein)
- OR a **hydrolysed protein diet** where proteins are broken down to fragments too small to trigger an immune response
- **Zero exceptions** — no treats, no flavoured medications, no table scraps for the entire trial period
- After 8–12 weeks, if signs improve significantly, the original diet is re-challenged. If signs return within 1–2 weeks, food allergy is confirmed.

## Management

Once the offending ingredient is identified, management is straightforward: lifelong avoidance. Many dogs do very well on a limited ingredient diet avoiding their trigger proteins. Some require a prescription hydrolysed diet long-term.

If you suspect your dog has food allergy, do not attempt the elimination trial without veterinary guidance — common mistakes (sneaking treats, not reading ingredient labels carefully) invalidate the entire trial and waste weeks.
