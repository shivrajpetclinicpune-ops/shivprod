Tick fever is one of the most common and dangerous tick-borne diseases affecting dogs in Pune, particularly during and after the monsoon season. At **Shivraj Pet Clinic & Lab**, we see a sharp spike in tick fever cases every July through October. Early detection and treatment are critical — untreated cases can progress to organ failure within days.

## What is Tick Fever?

Tick fever is a collective term for diseases caused by blood parasites and bacteria transmitted through tick bites. In India, the two most common forms are:

**Babesiosis** — caused by Babesia protozoa that invade and destroy red blood cells, leading to severe anaemia.

**Ehrlichiosis** — caused by Ehrlichia bacteria that infect white blood cells and platelets, weakening the immune system.

Both are transmitted primarily by the **brown dog tick (Rhipicephalus sanguineus)**, which thrives in Pune's warm, humid climate and survives indoors in carpets, sofas, and bedding.

## Symptoms of Tick Fever in Dogs

Symptoms typically appear 1–3 weeks after an infected tick bite:

- **High fever** — rectal temperature above 104°F (40°C), sometimes reaching 106°F
- **Severe lethargy** — the dog appears exhausted, loses interest in walks and play
- **Complete loss of appetite** — refusing food is a consistent early sign
- **Pale or yellowish gums** — indicates anaemia (Babesiosis) or jaundice
- **Swollen lymph nodes** — detectable under the jaw, behind the knees
- **Dark brown or bloody urine** — emergency sign indicating severe red blood cell destruction
- **Bleeding** — from gums, nose, or spontaneous bruising under the skin

**If you see dark urine, pale gums, or spontaneous bleeding — this is an emergency. Come in immediately.**

## Diagnosis at Shivraj Pet Clinic

We diagnose tick fever through:
- **Blood smear examination** — identifies Babesia parasites inside red blood cells under microscope
- **Complete Blood Count (CBC)** — detects anaemia, low platelet count, and white blood cell abnormalities
- **Rapid tick fever test** — available in-house, results in 15–20 minutes
- **PCR testing** — for accurate identification of the specific pathogen when needed

## Treatment

Treatment must begin immediately — never wait for "a day or two to see if it improves."

- **Anti-parasitic medication** (Imidocarb for Babesia) and **antibiotics** (Doxycycline for Ehrlichia) are the primary treatments
- **Supportive care** — IV fluids to maintain blood pressure and organ perfusion
- **Blood transfusion** — for severe anaemia with critically low packed cell volume
- **Liver and kidney support** — tick fever commonly causes secondary organ damage

Most dogs respond well to treatment when started in the acute phase. The chronic phase, where the infection has persisted untreated for weeks, is significantly harder to treat and can cause permanent organ damage.

## Prevention

- Consult your vet for appropriate tick prevention — options include spot-on treatments, oral tablets, and tick collars
- Check your dog daily for ticks, especially behind ears, between toes, and around the groin
- Remove any ticks found immediately using tweezers — grasp as close to the skin as possible and pull straight out
- Keep your home clean — vacuum regularly and wash pet bedding in hot water
