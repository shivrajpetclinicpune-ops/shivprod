Nutritional disease is the most common preventable cause of illness in pet birds. At **Shivraj Pet Clinic & Lab**, the majority of sick birds we see have some component of dietary deficiency — most often from a seed-only diet fed with the best intentions. Here is the complete guide to feeding your bird properly.

## Why Seed-Only Diets Fail

Seeds are high in fat and severely deficient in vitamins A, D3, calcium, and many essential amino acids. A bird fed seeds exclusively will appear healthy for years — then begin to show signs of nutritional disease: feather quality deterioration, respiratory problems, immune deficiency, and reproductive failure. By the time clinical signs appear, deficiency has often been present for months.

## The Ideal Bird Diet — By Species

### Budgerigars (Budgies)

**Pellets:** 50–60% of the diet. A quality small pellet (Harrison's, Zupreem) provides complete baseline nutrition.

**Fresh vegetables:** 20–30%. Leafy greens (spinach, coriander, methi), carrot, cucumber, and broccoli. Offer daily.

**Seeds:** 10–15% maximum. Millets and canary grass seed are preferred over sunflower and safflower.

**Fruits:** 5–10%. Small amounts of apple (no seeds), papaya, mango, and guava. Remove uneaten fresh food within 4 hours.

**Avoid:** Avocado, onion, garlic, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, and any pitted fruit seeds.

### Cockatiels

Cockatiels benefit from a similar diet to budgies but with slightly more variety and larger portions. They particularly enjoy fresh corn, sweet potato, and small cooked legumes. Cuttlebone should always be available as a calcium source and beak-conditioning tool.

**Key nutritional concerns:** Vitamin A deficiency is the most common problem in cockatiels. Signs include respiratory problems, eye discharge, and softening of the bones around the beak and choanal papillae. Dark orange and green vegetables prevent this.

### African Grey Parrots

African Greys have the highest calcium requirements of any commonly kept parrot species, due to their extraordinary susceptibility to hypocalcaemia (low blood calcium), which causes seizures.

**Diet:** High-quality pellets should form 60–70% of the diet. Calcium-rich foods — dark leafy greens, broccoli, almonds, and kale — are important supplements. Fresh vegetables should be offered daily. A balanced supplemental diet with calcium is essential.

**Avoid entirely:** Sunflower seeds (highly addictive and nutritionally poor), avocado, all members of the onion family, chocolate, and caffeine.

### Macaws and Large Parrots

Large parrots have higher caloric and fat requirements than smaller species. Their diet should include:
- High-quality large parrot pellets as the foundation
- A wide variety of fresh vegetables and fruits daily
- Nuts (almonds, walnuts, Brazil nuts) in moderation — 2–3 per day
- Cooked legumes and grains occasionally
- Fresh corn, sweet potato, and leafy greens regularly

## Converting a Seed-Addicted Bird to Pellets

Seed-addicted birds resist dietary change. Transition gradually:
- Mix 10% pellets with 90% seeds initially
- Very slowly increase pellet proportion over 4–6 weeks
- Never remove seeds abruptly — birds will starve rather than eat an unfamiliar food
- Offer pellets in the morning when the bird is hungriest
- Never place pellets in the seed dish — birds learn to throw them out

## Fresh Water

Change drinking water daily. Many birds dip food in water, contaminating it quickly. Water bowls should be cleaned with diluted vinegar weekly to prevent biofilm buildup.
