Pune's growing skyline means more cats are living in high-rise apartments with access to balconies and windows. At **Shivraj Pet Clinic & Lab**, we treat high-rise syndrome cases regularly — and the injuries, while survivable, are often serious and require immediate care.

## What is High-Rise Syndrome?

High-rise syndrome refers to the constellation of injuries that cats sustain after falling from significant heights — typically two storeys or more. Cats have a righting reflex that allows them to rotate mid-air and land feet-first. They also spread their body to increase drag and reduce fall speed. These adaptations mean cats can survive falls that would be fatal to humans.

However, surviving a fall does not mean surviving without injury.

## Why Do Cats Fall?

Cats have excellent balance but poor depth perception when looking straight down. They are also easily distracted — by a bird, an insect, or a sudden noise — and can lose their footing. Most falls happen through open windows without screens or from balcony railings. Cats in heat are particularly at risk as they may attempt to reach other cats outside.

## Common Injuries from High-Rise Falls

The impact of landing, even on four feet, causes predictable injury patterns:

- **Jaw fractures** — the chin often hits the ground as the cat's head snaps forward on impact
- **Split palate (cleft palate from trauma)** — the hard palate splits under the force of the jaw hitting the ground
- **Pneumothorax** — air leaks into the chest cavity, causing lung collapse
- **Limb fractures** — radius, ulna, and carpal bones absorb enormous impact
- **Internal bleeding** — rupture of the spleen, liver, or bladder
- **Thoracic contusions** — bruising of the lungs even without pneumothorax

Paradoxically, cats falling from greater heights (5+ floors) sometimes sustain fewer injuries than those falling from 2–3 floors. This is because cats reach terminal velocity and relax their muscles, spreading the impact across more of the body.

## What to Do If Your Cat Falls

1. **Do not assume they are fine because they are walking** — cats mask pain effectively, and internal injuries may not be visible
2. Confine your cat to a carrier or blanket — minimise movement
3. Come to Shivraj Pet Clinic **immediately** — even if the cat appears normal
4. Do not give any pain medication before being seen — it can mask symptoms and interfere with treatment

We will perform a thorough examination, chest X-rays to check for pneumothorax, abdominal assessment, and limb evaluation. Treatment depends on the injuries found.

## Prevention

- Install cat-safe mesh on all windows and balcony railings
- "Safety-proof" openings — cats can squeeze through surprisingly small gaps
- Supervise balcony access and never leave a cat alone on a high balcony
- Spay or neuter your cat — reduces the urge to reach other cats outside
